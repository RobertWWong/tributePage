Isaac Asimov: Putting the Science in Science Fiction

Here is the chronology of Isaac Asimov:

  • Born on January 2, 1920, in Petrovichi, Russia, to a ethnically Jewish Russian family.
  • Asimov's family came to the United States in 1923, East New York section of Brooklyn
  • After graduated from high school in 1935, he went to a branch of Columbia University in Downtown Brooklyn.
  • Obtained his BS in Chemistry at the Columbia University School of General Studies in 1939
  • After failing to secure admissions to a medical school, Asimov moved to a graduate program in Columbia, receiving an M.A. in Chemistry in 1941, and a Ph.D in Biochemistry in 1948.
  • Worked as a civilian at Philadelphia's Naval Air Experimental Station between 1942 to 1945
  • Drafted into the U.S. Army in 1945, but due to a bureaucratic foul-up was returned from a task force headed to participate in Operation Crossroads in 1946
  • Made into an associate professor in biochemistry in 1955
  • Stopped teaching in 1959 to pursue his career in writing.
  • Honored and promotoed to a full professor of biochemistry in 1979

"I'm on fire to explain, and happiest when it's something reasonably intricate which I can make clear step by step. It's the easiest way I can clarify things in my own mind."

In the early 60's, we had a few industrial robots, and some professors sought to form a learned society, to be called Robotology. I was aghast. The appellation for our discipline had been coined by Asimov in the 40's: Robotics. I am happy to say Asimov prevailed.
Joseph F. Engelberger Pioneer in industrial robotics; chairman, Transitions Research

There was a generation of scientists who are now between 40 and 60 who were tremendously influenced by his writing. It had adventure and it had science even though he sometimes did not understand what he was writing about. In a technical or scientific sense, it was well written and it had a sense of optimism. Intrinsic to his writing was the notion that there isn't anything that we can't master. His world view was that we can solve all the problems of the world.
Leon M. Lederman Winner, Nobel Prize in Physics; professor, University of Chicago

Isaac Asimov always encouraged his readers to try to see the world as comprehensible, to despise superstition and moral cowardice, and to think and speak clearly and simply. He was not falsely modest, but was always honest, unpretentious and humorous. To me he was among the finest of modern philosophers.
Marvin Minsky Artificial-intelligence pioneer; computer science professor, M.I.T.

When Isaac Asimov passed away, the world lost an unbridled dreamer and at the same time a sober scientist and analyst. Asimov merged a formidable literary talent with a deep understanding of science and its meaning. He did this not only in terms of technological progress, but also in terms of the development of our own personalities, penetrating a sometimes puzzling and horrifying world of contemporary science.
Vladimir P. Kartsev Director ,Untied Nations Publishing; ex-head, MIR Publishers, Moscow

The wonder of his generous -- torrential -- response to the public's increasingly anxious interest in science was his clarity and integrity. He leveled with his readers, never condescended and never overheated the story.
Gerard Piel Former president and publisher, Scientific American magazine
